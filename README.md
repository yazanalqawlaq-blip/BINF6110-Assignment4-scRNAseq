## Introduction
 
Influenza A virus (IAV) infects the upper respiratory tract through the nasal mucosa, which is the first barrier the virus encounters before it can spread to the lower airways (Iwasaki and Pillai, 2014). The goal here was to look at the early neutrophil transcriptomic response to IAV infection in the murine nasal respiratory mucosa using single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data from Kazer et al. (2024). Specifically, I compared neutrophils in the respiratory mucosa between naive (uninfected) mice and mice at 2 days post infection (D02), which corresponds to peak viral load and the first wave of innate immune cell recruitment.
 
The nasal mucosa contains a range of epithelial, immune, neuronal, and stromal cell types that coordinate the host response to infection. The composition and activation state of these populations shifts as the immune response moves through different phases, starting with innate recognition and neutrophil recruitment, followed by monocyte-derived macrophage accumulation, and eventually adaptive T cell responses (Kazer et al., 2024). Neutrophils are one of the first cell types to show up at the site of IAV infection, typically within hours of exposure (Tate et al., 2009). They help clear virus through phagocytosis, release of antimicrobial granule contents, reactive oxygen species production, and formation of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs). NETs are web-like structures made of chromatin and granule proteins that trap and neutralize viral particles (Brinkmann et al., 2004; Papayannopoulos, 2018). However, excessive neutrophil infiltration and activation can cause tissue damage and contribute to immunopathology, so influenza-associated lung injury can be severe even with successful viral clearance (Tate et al., 2011). This makes neutrophils worth profiling at the single-cell level during early infection.
 
scRNA-seq allows profiling of gene expression at the individual cell level, which matters in a tissue like the nasal mucosa where bulk RNA-seq would average across dozens of cell types and hide cell-type-specific responses (Jovic et al., 2022). The dataset used here was generated by Kazer et al. (2024), who built a spatiotemporal atlas of the murine nasal mucosa during primary IAV H1N1 PR8 infection. They sampled three tissue regions (respiratory mucosa, olfactory mucosa, and lateral nasal gland) at five timepoints (naive, 2, 5, 8, and 14 days post infection) with n=3 biological replicates per condition, for a total of 156,572 cells and 25,129 genes. I focused on the respiratory mucosa (RM) because that is where IAV replication is concentrated, and on D02 because it captures the neutrophil response before the broader myeloid and adaptive immune responses that develop at later timepoints.
 
For the computational pipeline, I used Seurat v5 (Hao et al., 2024) for data handling, quality control, normalization, clustering, and visualization. Seurat is standard for scRNA-seq analysis in R and covers everything from raw count matrices to annotation and differential expression. For batch correction, I used Harmony (Korsunsky et al., 2019) rather than Seurat's built-in CCA or RPCA integration methods. Harmony runs much faster on large datasets (149,125 cells in this case) and operates in the PCA embedding space rather than requiring identification of mutual nearest neighbors, which made it more practical for this dataset. CCA and RPCA perform comparably to Harmony in benchmarks (Tran et al., 2020), so this was mainly a computational decision. For differential expression, I used pseudobulk aggregation with DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014) rather than single-cell-level tests such as Seurat's default Wilcoxon test or MAST (Finak et al., 2015). Pseudobulk treats biological replicates rather than individual cells as the unit of analysis, which avoids the inflated false positive rates that occur when thousands of cells from the same sample are treated as independent observations (Squair et al., 2021; course lecture 18). Functional enrichment was performed with clusterProfiler (Wu et al., 2021), which I chose over alternatives such as DAVID or g:Profiler because it works directly in R, supports GSEA and ORA, and has a compareCluster() function for comparing upregulated and downregulated gene sets via the `compareCluster()` function.

## Methods
 
### Data Source and Experimental Design
 
The dataset was obtained as a pre-processed Seurat object provided by the course instructor, originally from Kazer et al. (2024). The experiment used C57BL/6 mice infected intranasally with IAV H1N1 PR8 to keep infection in the upper respiratory tract. Nasal mucosa tissue was collected from three anatomical regions at five timepoints: naive (uninfected), 2 days post infection (D02), 5 dpi (D05), 8 dpi (D08), and 14 dpi (D14), with three biological replicates per condition. Sequencing was done with 10x Genomics and processed through Cell Ranger for cell calling and gene quantification. The Seurat object had 156,572 cells and 25,129 genes with raw and normalized count matrices but no dimensionality reductions or cluster assignments.
 
### Quality Control and Filtering
 
Mitochondrial gene percentages were calculated using the `PercentageFeatureSet()` function with the pattern `^mt-` (lowercase, as mouse mitochondrial genes follow this naming convention). I looked at three QC metrics: number of genes detected per cell (nFeature_RNA), total UMI counts per cell (nCount_RNA), and mitochondrial percentage (percent.mt). Cells were filtered using two thresholds:
 
- **nFeature_RNA > 200:** This removes empty droplets and debris. The 1st percentile of the gene count distribution was 790, but some cells had as few as 60 detected genes, which are likely non-cellular barcodes. The 200-gene threshold is the standard default in Seurat (Hao et al., 2024) and works here because Cell Ranger already pre-filtered most low-quality barcodes.
- **percent.mt < 10:** This removes dying or damaged cells that lost cytoplasmic mRNA through membrane rupture but kept their mitochondrial transcripts. The 95th percentile of mitochondrial percentage was 9.8%, so this cuts roughly the top 5% of cells by mitochondrial content. 5% would have been too aggressive for nasal tissue since it has metabolically active glandular and neuronal cells with naturally higher mitochondrial gene expression.
 
No upper gene count filter was applied because the maximum nFeature_RNA in the dataset was only 3,892, which is well under the range (>6,000) where doublets usually show up in 10x Genomics data. After filtering, 149,125 cells were retained out of the original 156,572 (95.2%).
 
Approximately 36% of cells (54,087 out of 149,125) had blank `mouse_id` values in the metadata, so they could not be assigned to a specific biological replicate. I tagged these as "unassigned" and excluded them from pseudobulk DE, which needs replicate-level grouping, but kept them for clustering and visualization since the expression data itself is fine.
 
### Normalization, Integration, and Clustering
 
Gene expression counts were normalized using log normalization via `NormalizeData()` with default parameters (scale factor = 10,000). This normalizes each cell's total counts to 10,000 before log-transforming so cells with different sequencing depths are on a comparable scale. SCTransform would have been better for stabilizing variance across expression levels with regularized negative binomial regression (Hafemeister and Satija, 2019), but it was too memory-intensive to run locally on 149,125 cells. Log normalization is still what most published scRNA-seq studies use and it works fine for what I needed here.
 
The top 3,000 most variable genes were identified with `FindVariableFeatures()` using the variance-stabilizing transformation (vst) method. I chose 3,000 rather than the Seurat default of 2,000 because this dataset spans many distinct tissue types (neuronal, epithelial, immune, stromal) across three anatomical regions, so a larger feature set helps pick up the range of lineage-defining genes without adding too much noise. Expression values were scaled using `ScaleData()` with mitochondrial percentage regressed out via the `vars.to.regress` parameter, so residual mitochondrial variation does not influence clustering or PC structure.
 
PCA was run with 50 components using `RunPCA()`. An elbow plot was used to assess the number of informative dimensions. The curve flattened after about 30 PCs, so I kept 30 for downstream steps. Fewer PCs (e.g. 15-20) risks losing signal from rare cell types, and more (e.g. 40-50) would just add noise.
 
Batch effects across samples were corrected using Harmony v1.2.0 (Korsunsky et al., 2019), with `orig.ident` as the batch variable. Each `orig.ident` value represents a unique combination of tissue region, timepoint, and sequencing run, so it is the right grouping variable for integration. Harmony converged after 3 iterations. UMAP was computed on the Harmony-corrected embedding using 30 dimensions with default parameters (n.neighbors = 30, min.dist = 0.3). I compared pre- and post-integration UMAP plots to check that batch effects were corrected without losing biological structure.
 
Clustering was performed using shared nearest neighbor (SNN) graph construction followed by the Louvain algorithm via `FindNeighbors()` and `FindClusters()`. I tested three resolution values (0.3, 0.5, and 0.8) to see how cluster granularity changed. Resolution 0.3 produced 26 clusters, which merged some populations that should be separate. Resolution 0.8 produced 48 clusters, which over-split some populations into subsets that I could not tell apart by marker genes. Resolution 0.5 produced 38 clusters, which is close to what Kazer et al. reported (42 clusters) and gave enough granularity to separate real populations without over-fragmenting. I used Louvain rather than Leiden because it is the Seurat default and gives comparable results at this scale (Traag et al., 2019).
 
### Cluster Annotation
 
I annotated cell types manually using two approaches. First, I ran `FindAllMarkers()` with a Wilcoxon rank-sum test on each cluster to find genes differentially expressed in each cluster versus all others. To keep runtime reasonable on 149,125 cells I downsampled to 300 cells per cluster using `max.cells.per.ident = 300`. The `min.pct` parameter was set to 0.25, so a gene had to be detected in at least 25% of cells in the cluster to be tested, filtering out genes expressed in too few cells to be useful markers. The `logfc.threshold` was set to 0.5, stricter than the Seurat default of 0.25, to focus on genes with at least a 1.4-fold difference and cut down on weakly DE genes. The top 5 markers per cluster by log2 fold change were then examined for known cell-type-specific genes.
 
Second, I generated feature plots for canonical lineage markers across all clusters to double-check the marker gene assignments. Key markers used for annotation included Cd3d/Cd3e (T cells), Cd19/Cd79a (B cells), Cd68/Adgre1 (macrophages), S100a8/S100a9 (neutrophils), Ncr1/Klrb1c (NK cells), Ly6c2/Ccr2 (monocytes), Itgax/H2-Aa (dendritic cells), Epcam/Krt5 (epithelial/basal), Foxj1 (ciliated), Muc5b (secretory/goblet), Col1a1 (fibroblasts), Pecam1 (endothelial), Omp (olfactory neurons), Mki67 (proliferating), Trpm5/Il25 (tuft cells), and Krt13 (KNIIFE cells described by Kazer et al.). This gave 27 cell types across immune, epithelial, neuronal, and stromal lineages.
 
### Differential Expression Analysis
 
Pseudobulk differential expression was performed on neutrophils from the respiratory mucosa, comparing Naive versus D02. I only included cells with valid replicate IDs (non-blank mouse_id). For each biological replicate, raw UMI counts across all neutrophils belonging to that sample were summed using `GetAssayData()` on the counts layer, to get pseudobulk expression profiles. This produced 6 pseudobulk samples: 3 Naive (105, 65, and 63 cells from mice m1, m2, m3) and 3 D02 (231, 145, and 78 cells). Unequal cell numbers per replicate are normal in scRNA-seq and DESeq2's size factor normalization handles this by accounting for library size differences.
 
DESeq2 v1.46.0 (Love et al., 2014) was used for differential testing with the design formula `~ condition`, where Naive was set as the reference level. Genes with fewer than 10 counts in fewer than 2 samples were filtered out prior to analysis. This removes genes too lowly expressed to test reliably and reduces the multiple testing burden, improving power after p-value adjustment. After filtering, 5,379 genes remained. The Wald test was used to identify differentially expressed genes, and results were extracted with a Benjamini-Hochberg adjusted p-value threshold of 0.05. Positive log2 fold change values indicate higher expression at D02 relative to Naive.
 
### Functional Enrichment Analysis
 
Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was run using the `gseGO()` function from clusterProfiler v4.18.4 (Wu et al., 2021) on Gene Ontology Biological Process terms. Genes were ranked using a composite score of log2FoldChange multiplied by -log10(p-value), which accounts for both fold change direction and statistical significance. This is better than ranking by log2FC alone since it downweights genes with big fold changes but weak statistical support. Gene symbols were converted to Entrez IDs using `bitr()` from the org.Mm.eg.db annotation package (Carlson, 2019); 2.75% of gene IDs failed to map and were excluded. GSEA parameters included a minimum gene set size of 15 and maximum of 500 to drop gene sets that are too small for stable enrichment scores or too broad to be informative, and a p-value cutoff of 0.05.
 
Over-representation analysis (ORA) was performed separately on upregulated (padj < 0.05, log2FC > 1) and downregulated (padj < 0.05, log2FC < -1) gene sets using `compareCluster()` with `enrichGO()` for GO Biological Process terms. The log2FC threshold of 1 corresponds to a 2-fold change in expression, a standard cutoff for separating biologically meaningful changes from small-magnitude ones that happen to be significant. This lets me compare processes activated versus suppressed in neutrophils at D02 side by side. I provided all tested genes as the background universe to avoid the bias that comes from using the full genome. KEGG pathway enrichment was also run on the upregulated gene set using `enrichKEGG()` with organism code "mmu" for mouse, with p-value and q-value cutoffs of 0.05 and 0.2 respectively.
 
### Cell Composition Analysis
 
I calculated cell type proportions per replicate per timepoint in RM, using only cells with valid replicate IDs. For each replicate, the number of cells assigned to each cell type was divided by the total number of cells in that replicate to get proportions. I computed mean proportions and standard errors across the three replicates at each timepoint. I used stacked bar plots to show composition changes over the infection time course and line plots with error bars to track immune cell proportions over time. A separate stacked bar plot compared composition across all three tissue regions (RM, OM, LNG) to check that the cell type distributions matched known nasal mucosa anatomy.

The dataset had 156,572 cells before filtering. Median UMI count was 4,983 per cell (range 3,247 to 11,694) and median gene count was 1,954 (range 60 to 3,892). Mitochondrial percentage had a median of 2.9% with a max of 16.7%. After filtering for nFeature_RNA > 200 and percent.mt < 10, 149,125 cells (95.2%) were retained. QC violin plots showed that filtering removed low-quality outlier cells while keeping the bulk of the data across all timepoints (Figure 1).

### Cell Type Composition Over Infection
 
Cell type proportions in the RM changed a lot over the course of infection (Figure 5). Immune populations, especially neutrophils and monocytes, expanded at D02 and D05 as the innate response kicked in, which fits the expected stepwise immune response to IAV. By D08, macrophages and T cells became more prominent as the adaptive response developed, and by D14 the composition started shifting back toward the naive baseline.

### Differential Expression: Neutrophils in RM, Naive vs D02
 
Pseudobulk DE analysis found 736 differentially expressed genes (padj < 0.05) in RM neutrophils between Naive and D02. Of these, 299 were upregulated and 437 were downregulated at D02 relative to Naive. With a log2 fold change threshold of 1, there were 203 upregulated and 172 downregulated genes (Figure 7).
 
The top upregulated genes at D02 included S100a9 (log2FC = 1.30, padj = 5.3e-52) and S100a8 (log2FC = 0.96, padj = 2.4e-19), which encode the two subunits of calprotectin, a DAMP released during neutrophil activation and degranulation. Other highly upregulated genes included Slpi (log2FC = 2.74), a secretory leukocyte protease inhibitor with direct antiviral and antimicrobial activity; Mmp8 (log2FC = 2.85), a neutrophil collagenase stored in specific granules that helps with tissue migration; Tnfaip2 (log2FC = 1.76), a TNF-alpha and interferon-responsive gene involved in inflammatory signaling; and Ngp (log2FC = 2.40), a neutrophil granule protein. Among the top downregulated genes were Map1lc3a (log2FC = -2.97), Rplp1 (log2FC = -0.87), and several other ribosomal protein genes, pointing to a global drop in translational activity at D02.

## Discussion
 
The results here show that RM neutrophils go through major transcriptomic changes between the naive state and 2 days post IAV infection. The upregulation of antimicrobial effector genes alongside the downregulation of translational machinery fits with a shift from a circulating, biosynthetically active state to a fully activated effector phenotype. This has been seen in other contexts of neutrophil activation: mature neutrophils are terminally differentiated cells with short lifespans and low baseline transcriptional activity, and their antimicrobial functions rely mostly on pre-formed granule contents rather than de novo protein synthesis (Borregaard, 2010).
 
The top upregulated genes at D02 were S100a8 and S100a9, the two subunits of calprotectin. Calprotectin is one of the most abundant cytoplasmic proteins in neutrophils, making up roughly 40% of the total cytosolic protein content (Vogl et al., 2007). When neutrophils are activated, degranulate, or undergo NETosis, calprotectin gets released into the extracellular space where it acts as a damage-associated molecular pattern. Extracellular S100A8/A9 binds TLR4 and RAGE receptors on neighboring immune cells, triggering NF-kB-dependent production of pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha, IL-6, and IL-1beta, which amplifies the local inflammatory response (Vogl et al., 2007). For respiratory viral infection, elevated calprotectin levels have been used as a biomarker for disease severity: Silvin et al. (2020) showed that serum calprotectin levels distinguished severe from mild COVID-19 cases and correlated with dysregulated myeloid cell responses. Our data is from a mouse IAV model and not human SARS-CoV-2, but the strong upregulation of both S100a8 and S100a9 at D02 still fits with calprotectin release being a conserved part of the early neutrophil response to respiratory viral infection. Kazer et al. (2024) similarly identified interferon-responsive neutrophil subsets expressing high levels of S100a8/a9 as among the first immune populations to expand in the RM during primary IAV infection.
 
Slpi (secretory leukocyte protease inhibitor) was the second most significantly upregulated gene at D02 (log2FC = 2.74, padj = 1.2e-41). Slpi is a serine protease inhibitor with two protective functions at the mucosal surface. First, it inhibits neutrophil elastase and cathepsin G, which limits protease-mediated tissue damage that would otherwise come from neutrophil degranulation at the infection site. Second, Slpi has direct antiviral activity against influenza: Kido et al. (1999) showed that Slpi inhibits the host tryptase Clara, which cleaves influenza hemagglutinin (HA) into its fusogenic form needed for viral entry. By blocking HA cleavage, Slpi can directly reduce viral infectivity in the airway epithelium. So the upregulation of Slpi in D02 neutrophils is both a tissue-protective mechanism to limit collateral damage from the neutrophil response and a direct antiviral defense. This matters especially in the nasal mucosa, where the epithelial barrier needs to stay intact to maintain airway function.
 
Mmp8 (matrix metalloproteinase 8, also known as neutrophil collagenase) was one of the most strongly upregulated genes (log2FC = 2.85, padj = 2.6e-18). MMP8 is stored in the specific (secondary) granules of neutrophils and gets released upon activation. Its main function is degradation of type I, II, and III collagens in the extracellular matrix, which helps neutrophils transmigrate through the basement membrane and interstitial tissue to get to the infection site (Borregaard, 2010). The strong upregulation of Mmp8 at D02, along with the upregulation of Plek (pleckstrin, log2FC = 1.52), which is involved in granule secretion and integrin activation, points to neutrophils that are actively degranulating and migrating through the RM tissue toward virus-infected epithelial cells. This fits with the known kinetics of neutrophil recruitment during influenza, where neutrophils start accumulating in the nasal mucosa within 24-48 hours of infection (Tate et al., 2009).
 
The KEGG enrichment result for neutrophil extracellular trap formation as a significantly enriched pathway ties together several of the gene-level findings. NETs are made of decondensed chromatin decorated with antimicrobial proteins from neutrophil granules, including calprotectin (S100A8/A9), myeloperoxidase, and neutrophil elastase. When released into the extracellular space, NETs can physically trap viral particles and expose them to high local concentrations of antimicrobial proteins, helping with viral clearance (Brinkmann et al., 2004). NET formation during influenza infection has been shown both in vitro and in mouse models, where NETs have been found in the airway lumen during acute infection (Narasaraju et al., 2011). That said, excessive NETosis is also linked to tissue injury and thrombosis, and Narasaraju et al. (2011) showed that NET-associated histones contribute to alveolar epithelial damage during severe influenza pneumonia. In our data, the upregulation of S100a8/a9, Ngp (neutrophil granule protein), and the enrichment of the NET formation pathway at D02 suggests that RM neutrophils are actively forming NETs as part of their initial antiviral response in the nasal mucosa.
 
The downregulation of ribosomal protein genes, cytoplasmic translation, and oxidative phosphorylation at D02 is worth discussing because it seems counterintuitive for cells that are clearly activated. But this fits with what is known about neutrophil biology. Unlike macrophages or T cells, which ramp up transcription and translation upon activation to produce cytokines and effector molecules, mature neutrophils rely mostly on pre-formed granule contents for their immediate antimicrobial functions (Borregaard, 2010). The drop in translational gene expression at D02 likely reflects the fact that these neutrophils have already left the bone marrow with a full set of granule proteins and are now deploying them rather than making new ones. The downregulation of oxidative phosphorylation genes supports this: activated neutrophils are known to shift from oxidative phosphorylation toward glycolytic metabolism during respiratory burst and NETosis, since glycolysis can sustain ATP production in the hypoxic conditions of inflamed tissue and also feeds the pentose phosphate pathway, which generates the NADPH needed for the oxidative burst (Rodriguez-Espinosa et al., 2015).
 
The composition analysis across RM timepoints recovered the expected stepwise pattern of immune cell recruitment during primary IAV infection. Neutrophils peaked early at D02 (concurrent with peak viral load), followed by monocyte accumulation at D05 (myeloid recruitment phase), macrophage expansion at D08, and T cell infiltration starting at D08 (adaptive response). This sequence where neutrophils provide the first line of defense before handing off to monocyte-derived macrophages and then T cells is well-characterized in the innate-to-adaptive immune transition during influenza infection (Iwasaki and Pillai, 2014; Tate et al., 2009; Tate et al., 2011). The fact that I recovered this temporal pattern from compositional analysis of the scRNA-seq data validates both the cluster annotation and the quality of the dataset.

## Limitations
 
1. **Pseudobulk sample size.** The pseudobulk DE analysis used 3 replicates per condition (3 Naive vs 3 D02). This is the minimum for DESeq2, so power to detect small effect sizes is limited and variance estimates are less stable. More biological replicates would help, especially for genes near the significance threshold.
 
2. **Unassigned cells.** About 36% of cells had blank mouse_id values and were excluded from pseudobulk DE. If these cells are not randomly distributed across conditions or if they represent a specific subset of cell states, this could bias the composition analysis or reduce the cells available for DE testing.
 
3. **Log normalization.** SCTransform would have been better here since it handles the mean-variance relationship in UMI count data more appropriately (Hafemeister and Satija, 2019), but it was not feasible to run on 149,125 cells with the hardware I had. Log normalization is still what most published scRNA-seq studies use.
 
4. **Cluster resolution.** Resolution 0.5 gave 38 clusters, which separated major cell types well but may have merged finer subpopulations. Kazer et al. (2024) identified ISG-high versus ISG-low neutrophil subsets in their analysis, and these could be merged in our clustering. Subclustering the neutrophil cluster at a higher resolution could pick up additional heterogeneity.
 
---
 
## Conclusion
 
This analysis looked at the early neutrophil response to IAV infection in the murine nasal respiratory mucosa using scRNA-seq data from Kazer et al. (2024). Pseudobulk DE between Naive and D02 found 736 DE genes, with strong upregulation of calprotectin subunits (S100a8/a9), the antimicrobial protease inhibitor Slpi, the tissue remodeling enzyme Mmp8, and other neutrophil granule proteins, alongside downregulation of ribosomal and translational machinery. GSEA and KEGG enrichment backed up the activation of innate immune defense pathways and neutrophil extracellular trap formation. Composition analysis across all five timepoints recovered the expected stepwise pattern of immune cell infiltration during primary IAV infection. Put together, these results give a detailed look at the transcriptomic state of neutrophils during the earliest phase of the nasal immune response to influenza, fitting a model where newly recruited neutrophils rapidly deploy pre-formed granule contents and form NETs while shutting down biosynthetic pathways.
